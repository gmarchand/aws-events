# AI-Driven Automation for Modern Operations

## Session Overview

This AWS re:Invent 2025 session, presented by PagerDuty in partnership with AWS, examines how AI-powered automation is fundamentally transforming digital operations across enterprises. Drawing on real-world implementations at organizations such as Warner Brothers Discovery, the discussion centers on how agentic AI is reshaping the way operations teams handle incidents, reduce toil, and free engineers to focus on higher-value strategic work. The session frames AI agents not as a replacement for human expertise, but as collaborators that operate across the entire incident lifecycle—from prevention and detection through diagnosis, remediation, and post-incident learning.

## The Operational Transformation Through Agentic AI

### Reducing Toil Across the Incident Lifecycle

Modern operations teams spend significant effort on repetitive, time-consuming activities that drain capacity and slow innovation. AI agents are now stepping into many of these roles by adjusting on-call schedules, diagnosing issues as they emerge, scribing the details of active incidents, executing remediation playbooks, and standardizing how teams respond to recurring problems. By automating these tasks, organizations like Warner Brothers Discovery report meaningful reductions in operational burden, allowing engineers to redirect their energy toward improving system resilience and building new capabilities rather than fighting fires.

### A Framework for Three Types of Operational Work

A central theme of the presentation is a practical framework for understanding where agentic AI delivers the most value. Operational work falls into three categories: well-understood tasks with clear patterns and known solutions, partially understood tasks where context and judgment matter but precedents exist, and novel or major events that require deep human expertise and creative problem solving. Well-understood work is ideal for full agent execution because the inputs, outputs, and remediation steps are predictable. Partially understood work benefits from AI as an accelerator—surfacing context, suggesting actions, and drafting responses while a human validates the path forward. Novel and major incidents remain firmly in the human domain, though even here AI provides value by assembling timelines, summarizing telemetry, and reducing cognitive load during high-pressure moments.

### Evaluating Where Generative AI Adds Value

The session emphasizes that not every task should be handed to an agent, and choosing wisely is essential to building trust and demonstrating impact. Teams are encouraged to evaluate candidate tasks based on the clarity of their inputs, the reversibility of their actions, the availability of high-quality data, and the consequences of errors. Generative AI shines in summarization, communication, knowledge retrieval, and drafting structured responses, while deterministic automation remains better suited for actions requiring strict reliability. Recognizing this boundary helps organizations deploy AI responsibly and build a sustainable operating model where humans and agents each contribute their strengths.

## Outcomes and Strategic Implications

Organizations that adopt this layered approach to AI-driven operations are seeing faster incident resolution, more consistent response quality, and improved engineer satisfaction as toil decreases. The broader implication is a shift in how operations teams define their value: rather than measuring success by hours spent responding to alerts, leading teams now focus on continuous improvement, system reliability engineering, and innovation velocity. The session closes by encouraging attendees to identify their highest-toil workflows, map them against the three-category framework, and begin introducing AI agents incrementally—building confidence through measurable wins before expanding to more complex scenarios. The future of operations, the presenters argue, is one where AI handles the routine so humans can master the exceptional.
