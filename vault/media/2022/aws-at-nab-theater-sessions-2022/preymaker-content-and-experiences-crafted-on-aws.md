# Preymaker: Content and Experiences Crafted on AWS

## Reimagining Creative Production in the Cloud

Preymaker, a creative studio led by Chief Creative Angus Kneale, has fundamentally transformed how high-end visual content is produced by building its operations on Amazon Web Services. Rather than tethering artists to physical workstations housed within a traditional facility, Preymaker runs its production pipeline — including the demanding Autodesk Flame compositing and finishing software — on Amazon Elastic Compute Cloud (Amazon EC2) instances. This shift represents a deliberate move away from the legacy infrastructure model that has long defined post-production studios, where powerful hardware and proprietary networks dictated where and how creative work could happen.

By running Flame in the cloud, Preymaker eliminates the geographic and logistical constraints that traditionally limited creative collaboration. Artists no longer need to travel to a specific suite or office to access the tools and storage required for finishing work, and the studio gains the elasticity to scale resources up or down as project demands fluctuate.

## Running Autodesk Flame on Amazon EC2

### Technical Approach and Workflow

The core of Preymaker's innovation lies in operating Autodesk Flame, one of the most performance-intensive applications in the visual effects and finishing industry, on EC2 instances rather than on dedicated on-premises workstations. This configuration allows the studio to provision high-performance compute capacity on demand and to allocate it dynamically to artists wherever they happen to be working. AWS infrastructure provides the GPU power, networking throughput, and storage performance that Flame historically required from purpose-built hardware, while removing the capital expense and maintenance burden associated with maintaining a fleet of physical machines.

### Impact on Artists and Clients

Kneale describes the ability to run Flame from almost anywhere as a game-changer for both Preymaker and its clients. Artists can log in remotely and continue working at the same fidelity they would expect from a tethered workstation, which expands the talent pool the studio can draw from and supports more flexible working arrangements. For clients, this translates into faster turnaround, easier review collaboration across regions, and a production model that adapts to the realities of modern, distributed creative teams. The outcome is a studio capable of delivering premium content and experiences with greater agility, resilience, and creative reach than the traditional facility-based model allows.
