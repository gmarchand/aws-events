# How Generative AI is Changing the Game: Insights from NAB 2024

This NAB 2024 main stage session, moderated by Thursday Night Football broadcaster Kaylee Hartung, brought together leaders from AWS, the NFL, and the PGA TOUR to explore how generative AI is reshaping sports, media, and entertainment. The conversation moved from foundational technology strategy to concrete, real-world deployments already changing how fans experience live sports.

## The AWS Approach to Generative AI

### A Foundation Built for Choice and Scale

Dr. Matt Wood, VP of AI Products at AWS, framed AWS's generative AI strategy around giving customers flexibility rather than locking them into a single model or workflow. He emphasized that no single foundation model will dominate every use case, which is why AWS invests in a layered stack — from custom silicon like Trainium and Inferentia at the infrastructure layer, to Amazon Bedrock for accessing a broad selection of foundation models, to ready-made applications such as Amazon Q at the top of the stack. Wood argued that media and sports organizations need this optionality because their workflows span everything from highlight generation to metadata enrichment to fan-facing personalization, and each task benefits from a different model profile in terms of cost, latency, and accuracy.

Wood also stressed the importance of data privacy and customization. Sports leagues sit on decades of proprietary footage, statistics, and editorial content, and the value of generative AI is unlocked only when those datasets can be combined securely with foundation models through techniques like retrieval-augmented generation and fine-tuning. AWS's position, he said, is to make that combination safe, performant, and cost-effective at scale.

## Real-World Applications in Sports

### Transforming the NFL Fan and Operations Experience

Aaron Amendolia, Deputy CIO of the NFL, described how the league is applying generative AI across both fan-facing products and internal operations. On the fan side, the NFL is using AI to accelerate the creation of personalized highlight reels, surface storylines that would otherwise take editors hours to assemble, and improve the speed at which content reaches social and digital platforms. Behind the scenes, generative AI is helping with everything from officiating support tools to player health and safety analysis, where computer vision and language models combine to extract insights from massive volumes of game footage. Amendolia underscored that the NFL's priority is augmenting human decision-makers — coaches, officials, broadcasters, and editors — rather than replacing them, and that trust and accuracy are non-negotiable when deploying AI in a live broadcast environment.

### Reinventing the PGA TOUR Viewing Experience

Scott Gutterman, SVP of Digital Operations for the PGA TOUR, explained how the TOUR has used AWS to tackle one of golf's defining challenges: covering 144 players across 18 holes simultaneously. Generative AI now helps the PGA TOUR produce automated shot-level commentary, generate natural-language summaries of player performance, and deliver personalized streams so fans can follow their favorite players without waiting for traditional broadcast cuts. Gutterman highlighted features that translate dense statistical data into conversational insights, making the sport more accessible to casual viewers while giving die-hard fans deeper analytical context. He noted that these capabilities would have been economically impossible to produce manually at the scale the TOUR requires.

### Strategic Partnerships and the Broader Sports Landscape

Julie Souza, AWS's Global Leader for Strategic Business Development in Sports, situated these examples within a wider industry shift. She explained that leagues, broadcasters, and streaming services are converging on similar generative AI use cases — automated highlights, multilingual commentary, metadata tagging of archives, and personalized fan engagement — and that AWS is partnering across the ecosystem to make these capabilities reusable. Souza pointed to the importance of unlocking historical archives, where generative AI can index and contextualize decades of footage that was previously inaccessible, opening new revenue streams through licensing, nostalgia content, and data-driven storytelling.

## The Road Ahead for AI in Live Production

### Cloud-Based Production and Personalization at Scale

The panel converged on a shared view that the future of live sports production is cloud-native and deeply personalized. Wood and Souza both pointed to the shift from one-size-fits-all broadcasts toward dynamically assembled experiences where each viewer might receive different camera angles, commentary styles, languages, or statistical overlays generated in near real time. Amendolia and Gutterman agreed that this shift depends on cloud infrastructure capable of handling generative workloads at broadcast latency, and on disciplined data governance so that leagues retain control of their intellectual property.

### Responsible Deployment and Human Creativity

A recurring theme was the responsible introduction of generative AI into workflows that millions of fans depend on. The panelists agreed that the technology is most powerful when it amplifies human creativity — freeing editors, producers, and analysts from repetitive tasks so they can focus on storytelling — rather than attempting to automate the craft entirely. The session closed on the consensus that generative AI will not replace the magic of live sports, but will make it more immersive, more personalized, and more globally accessible than ever before.
