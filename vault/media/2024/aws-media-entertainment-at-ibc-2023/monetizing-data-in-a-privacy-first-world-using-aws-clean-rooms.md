# Monetizing Data in a Privacy-First World Using AWS Clean Rooms

## Reframing Privacy Constraints as Business Opportunity

The session opens by addressing a pivotal moment in the advertising industry, where tightening privacy regulations, the deprecation of third-party cookies, and shifting consumer expectations are reshaping how media companies operate. Rather than treating these changes as threats, the speakers position the current climate as a strategic opportunity for broadcasters and advertisers to future-proof their operations. The central thesis is that privacy and monetization are not opposing forces; with the right architecture, organizations can simultaneously protect consumer data and unlock greater value from it. AWS Clean Rooms emerges as the foundational technology enabling this dual outcome, allowing multiple parties to collaborate on data without exposing or sharing the underlying records.

## TV New Zealand's Privacy-Focused Advertising Solution

### Building a Future-Proof Data Collaboration Model

TV New Zealand, as a leading national broadcaster, shares how it is reengineering its advertising business around a privacy-first foundation. The broadcaster recognized that its first-party audience data — derived from streaming services, registered users, and content engagement — represented one of its most strategic assets, but only if it could be activated in ways that respected viewer privacy and complied with evolving regulations. By adopting AWS Clean Rooms, TVNZ established a secure environment where it can collaborate with advertisers and partners on matched audience insights without ever transferring raw personally identifiable information. This approach allows the broadcaster to demonstrate audience value to advertisers, support more precise campaign targeting, and maintain the trust of its viewers, all while reducing the operational and legal risks tied to traditional data-sharing methods.

### Operational and Strategic Outcomes

The implementation enables TVNZ to move away from reliance on third-party identifiers and instead build durable, consent-based advertising products grounded in its own data. The broadcaster frames this work as essential to long-term competitiveness, ensuring it can continue to serve advertisers effectively as the open web's identity infrastructure erodes. The session emphasizes that the project is as much a cultural and commercial transformation as a technical one, requiring alignment between data, ad sales, engineering, and legal teams.

## Comscore's Role in Measurement and Transaction

### Enabling Planning, Buying, and Measurement in Clean Rooms

Comscore joins the discussion to explain how measurement providers fit into this privacy-safe ecosystem. Traditionally, measurement has depended on cross-referencing audience data across publishers and advertisers in ways that are increasingly difficult under modern privacy frameworks. Comscore describes how it is adapting its services to operate inside clean room environments, allowing broadcasters and advertisers to plan campaigns against verified audiences, transact with confidence in the underlying data, and measure outcomes — all without raw data leaving each party's secure boundary. This positions clean rooms not just as a data-sharing tool but as the new substrate for the advertising transaction itself.

### Industry Implications

The collaboration between TVNZ, Comscore, and AWS illustrates a broader template for the media industry. By combining a broadcaster's first-party data, a measurement partner's independent verification, and AWS Clean Rooms as the neutral technical layer, the parties demonstrate that the full advertising value chain — from planning through measurement — can function effectively without compromising consumer privacy. The session closes with the message that organizations that invest now in clean room capabilities will be best positioned to lead as the industry transitions fully into a privacy-first era.
