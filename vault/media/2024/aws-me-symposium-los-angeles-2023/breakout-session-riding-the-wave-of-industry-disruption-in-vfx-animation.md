# Riding the Wave of Industry Disruption in VFX & Animation

## Overview of a Sector in Transition

The visual effects and animation industry stands at a pivotal moment, confronting a convergence of disruptive forces that are fundamentally reshaping how studios operate, hire, and deliver creative work. This breakout session brought together perspectives from across the production landscape to examine how studios are navigating the aftermath of the Hollywood writers strike, the rapid emergence of generative AI, and a wave of consolidation that has redrawn the competitive map. The discussion centered on practical responses to these pressures and the strategic decisions studios must make to remain viable and creatively ambitious in an uncertain environment.

## Forces Reshaping Production

### The Strike Aftermath and Industry Consolidation

The writers strike triggered ripple effects that extended far beyond writers' rooms, disrupting production pipelines and forcing VFX and animation studios to reassess their operating models. In its wake, the industry has seen accelerated studio acquisitions and significant workforce consolidations, as larger players absorb smaller shops and streamline overlapping capabilities. These shifts have compressed margins and intensified competition for high-value projects, pushing studios to differentiate through specialization, efficiency, and creative excellence rather than scale alone.

### Generative AI as Both Threat and Opportunity

Generative AI emerged as the most consequential technological shift discussed, with the potential to compress timelines, reduce costs on certain tasks, and democratize aspects of content creation that were previously the exclusive domain of skilled artists. Studios are actively integrating AI tools into pipelines for tasks such as concept iteration, asset generation, rotoscoping, and look development, while wrestling with the cultural, legal, and ethical implications. The conversation acknowledged anxiety within creative workforces but framed AI as a tool that, properly deployed, augments rather than replaces artistry — provided studios invest in retraining and clear governance.

### Global Talent and Skills Gaps

Access to global talent has become both easier and more competitive in a remote-enabled world, allowing studios to draw on artists and technicians from emerging production hubs while also exposing skills gaps in areas such as AI-assisted workflows, real-time engines, and cloud-native production. Studios are responding by investing in upskilling programs, partnering with educational institutions, and rethinking traditional career paths to ensure their workforces can keep pace with technological change.

## Powering the Industry Forward

The session closed with a forward-looking discussion on how the industry can convert disruption into momentum. Studios that embrace cloud-based production infrastructure, integrate AI thoughtfully into creative pipelines, and commit to continuous workforce development are best positioned to thrive. Collaboration across the ecosystem — between studios, technology providers, and talent — was identified as essential, as no single organization can solve these challenges alone. The overarching message was one of cautious optimism: while the disruption is real and the pressures significant, the studios that adapt their business models, invest in their people, and harness new technologies stand to emerge stronger, with greater creative reach and operational resilience than before.
