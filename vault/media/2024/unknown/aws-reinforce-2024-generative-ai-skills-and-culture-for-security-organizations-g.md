# Building Generative AI Skills and Culture in Security Organizations

## Introduction to the Challenge

The rapid emergence of generative AI has placed security organizations in a difficult position. Teams must now evolve beyond their traditional security-first mindset to also embrace an AI/ML-first culture, all while ensuring that safety and risk management remain central to their operations. This lightning talk from AWS re:Inforce 2024 addresses how an AWS security team approached this challenge by upskilling not only their technical builders but every member of the organization, regardless of role.

## A Mental Model for Generative AI Upskilling

### Broadening the Definition of Who Needs AI Skills

A core insight from the session is that generative AI literacy cannot be confined to engineers and data scientists. Security organizations include program managers, analysts, compliance specialists, and leadership, all of whom interact with AI-driven workflows and decisions. The AWS security team recognized that creating a truly AI-fluent organization required investing in every individual, ensuring that conversations about generative AI risks, opportunities, and applications could happen at every level. By doing so, the team avoided the common pitfall of leaving non-technical staff behind, which would otherwise create blind spots in security posture and slow the adoption of AI-enabled tooling.

### Mechanisms and Practices for Cultural Transformation

The team adopted concrete mechanisms to grow generative AI capabilities throughout the organization. These included structured learning paths tailored to different roles, hands-on experimentation opportunities that allowed team members to apply generative AI to real security problems, and forums where employees could share lessons learned and emerging use cases. Leadership played a critical role by setting expectations, modeling curiosity, and providing the time and resources necessary for learning. The cultural shift was reinforced by treating generative AI not as a separate initiative but as an integrated part of how the security team works, thinks, and solves problems.

### Adapting the Approach to Other Organizations

The speaker emphasized that the framework presented is meant to be adapted rather than copied wholesale. Each security organization has its own maturity level, risk profile, and team composition, and the upskilling strategy should reflect those realities. By starting with a clear mental model—identifying who needs what skills, what mechanisms will deliver them, and how culture will sustain the change—security leaders can construct a tailored roadmap. Examples shared during the talk illustrate how AWS structured its own journey, providing a practical reference point for organizations beginning or accelerating their own transformation.

## Key Takeaways and Outcomes

The central outcome of the session is a transferable approach for security leaders who must balance the urgency of AI adoption with the discipline of strong security practice. By investing broadly across roles, embedding generative AI into everyday workflows, and using deliberate mechanisms to drive learning, security organizations can build both the skills and the culture needed to thrive. The talk reinforces that generative AI fluency is now a foundational capability for security teams, and that intentional, organization-wide upskilling is the path to making that capability real.
