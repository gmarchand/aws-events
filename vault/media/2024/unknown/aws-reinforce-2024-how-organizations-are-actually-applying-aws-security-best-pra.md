# How Organizations Are Actually Applying AWS Security Best Practices

## Overview of the Research Initiative

This lightning talk from AWS re:Inforce 2024 presents the findings of a collaborative research study conducted by Security-JAWS, the Japan AWS User Group focused on security. The study set out to address a fundamental question that many organizations struggle with: how do real-world AWS security implementations compare against published best practices? By surveying organizations across Japan and South Korea, the researchers sought to establish a tangible security baseline that practitioners could use as a meaningful reference point when evaluating their own environments.

The motivation behind the research stems from the recognition that while AWS publishes extensive guidance through resources such as the Well-Architected Framework and the security pillar documentation, organizations often lack visibility into how their peers are actually implementing these recommendations. This gap between prescriptive guidance and operational reality creates uncertainty for security teams attempting to prioritize investments and justify decisions to leadership.

## Key Findings and Regional Insights

### Survey Methodology and Participation

The Security-JAWS team gathered responses from a cross-section of organizations operating AWS workloads in Japan and South Korea, capturing data on the security controls, services, and practices they have adopted. The questions were designed to map directly to widely recognized AWS security best practices, allowing the researchers to measure adoption rates across categories such as identity and access management, logging and monitoring, data protection, incident response, and network security. By focusing on two geographically and culturally proximate but operationally distinct markets, the study offers comparative insights into how regional factors influence security maturity.

### Adoption Patterns and Gaps

The findings reveal both areas of strong alignment with AWS recommendations and notable gaps where adoption lags behind guidance. Foundational controls, particularly those tied to identity management and basic logging, tend to see broader implementation, reflecting their visibility in compliance frameworks and their relative ease of deployment. More advanced practices, including automated remediation, comprehensive threat detection coverage, and mature incident response workflows, show lower adoption rates, indicating opportunities for organizations to mature their postures. The comparison between Japanese and South Korean respondents also surfaces subtle differences shaped by regulatory environments, organizational culture, and the maturity of local cloud adoption.

## Takeaways and Recommendations for Strengthening Security Posture

The session translates the research findings into practical guidance for organizations seeking to improve their AWS security implementations. Practitioners are encouraged to use the baseline data as a benchmarking tool, identifying where their current controls align with peer organizations and where meaningful gaps exist. The researchers emphasize that closing these gaps does not require wholesale transformation but rather a disciplined focus on adopting the higher-impact controls that remain underutilized across the surveyed population.

Beyond the specific findings, the talk underscores the broader value of community-driven research within user groups like Security-JAWS. By pooling anonymized data and sharing results openly, the AWS user community can develop shared reference points that complement official AWS guidance. Organizations are encouraged to participate in similar studies, contribute their experiences, and use the resulting insights to drive evidence-based conversations about security investment with engineering teams and executive stakeholders.
